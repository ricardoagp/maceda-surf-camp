Portugal has become the default answer to the question "where should I go on a surf trip in Europe?" — and for good reason. Over 800 kilometres of Atlantic coastline. Consistent swell that arrives from October through March and doesn't fully switch off even in summer. A staggering variety of breaks, from the mellow beach breaks of the north to the world-class reef setups around Ericeira. Cheap flights from almost every European city. Food that punches so far above its weight that you'll gain a kilo just reading about it.

The result is that surf camps have multiplied. There are dozens scattered along the coast, from the Minho in the north to the Algarve in the south. Some are genuinely brilliant. Some are a bunk bed, a borrowed wetsuit and a hangover. The challenge isn't deciding whether to go to Portugal — it's figuring out which camp is actually worth your week off.

This guide is our honest attempt to help. We run a surf camp ourselves (Maceda Surf Camp, northern Portugal — we'll get to it), so we're not pretending to be neutral. But we've also been in this industry long enough to know what makes a camp good and what makes one forgettable. We'll lay it out straight and let you decide.

## How to choose a surf camp (what actually matters)

Before you get lost in Instagram feeds and promotional videos, think about five things. They sound obvious, but most people skip them and end up at a camp that doesn't match what they actually wanted.

**Location matters more than you think.** Not just "which country" or "which town" — but what kind of setting do you want to wake up in? Some camps are in the middle of a town with bars and restaurants on the doorstep. Others are in the middle of nowhere, surrounded by forest or farmland. Both are valid. The question is which version of a surf trip you're imagining. If you want to surf all day and go out all night, a rural eco-camp will bore you. If you want silence, nature and genuine disconnection, a hostel in a party town will exhaust you.

**The waves near the camp shape the experience.** A camp on a beach with mellow, forgiving waves is perfect for [beginners](/journal/surfing-northern-portugal-for-beginners-the-ultimate-guide/) but frustrating for intermediates who want to be pushed. A camp near a powerful reef break is exciting for experienced surfers but terrifying (and potentially dangerous) for someone who's never stood on a board. Ask about the specific breaks the camp uses, not just "we have great waves."

**Group size is everything for learning.** A surf lesson with four students and one instructor is a completely different experience from a lesson with twelve students and one instructor. In the smaller group, you get personal feedback, your mistakes get corrected in real time, and you catch three times as many waves. In the big group, you're essentially teaching yourself while someone in a rashguard watches from the channel. Ask about instructor-to-student ratios before you book. Anything above 1:6 should raise questions.

**The social element is usually what people remember most.** The waves are why you go. The people are why you come back. Camps that create genuine community — through communal meals, shared activities, bonfires, small group sizes — tend to produce the kind of trip where you leave with friends you'll visit in other countries. Camps that function more like hotels (check in, take a lesson, go back to your room) can feel isolating, especially if you're travelling alone.

**Sustainability is worth considering.** Surfing depends on healthy oceans. Camps that take environmental responsibility seriously — through building materials, waste reduction, community involvement, educational initiatives — are doing something that matters. It's also a signal: a camp that cares about its environment tends to care about its guests too.

## The best surf camps in Portugal for 2026

### 1. Maceda Surf Camp — our pick (yes, we're biased)

**Where:** Maceda, Ovar — 40 minutes south of Porto, on Portugal's Costa Verde
**Best for:** Nature lovers, eco-conscious travellers, beginners to intermediates, solo travellers looking for genuine connection
**Price:** From €290/week (dorm, with lessons, breakfast, activities and equipment included)

Full disclosure: this is our camp. We're going to tell you why we think it's special, and you can weigh that against the fact that we're obviously not objective.

[Maceda Surf Camp](/) was founded in 2011 by João Diogo, a Portuguese surfer who started riding waves around Porto back in 1980 and went on to establish Porto's first surf school in 1994. He found a 5,000 m² plot of land next to a pine forest, near a wild beach that nobody was surfing, and built a camp from scratch using about [70% recycled and reclaimed materials](/concept/). Salvaged wood. Recycled metal. Repurposed shipping containers. Even old surfboards and wetsuits used as insulation in the walls. It wasn't designed to be a business. It was designed to be a place where people could live simply, close to the ocean and close to nature.

Today the camp is run by Kate and André. Kate grew up near Maceda, has the kind of warmth that makes solo travellers feel at home within hours, and handles the day-to-day with a calm efficiency that belies how much work it takes to run a place like this. André teaches surfing and surfskate, builds legendary bonfires, and brings an energy that makes you want to try things you'd normally talk yourself out of.

The [surf programme](/surf/) runs on the beaches between Maceda and Cortegaça — 12 kilometres of wild, sandy coast backed by pine forest, with barely anyone in the water. Our instructors check conditions every morning and take guests to whichever peak is working best. Groups are small. The approach is patient, anti-judgement, rooted in over 30 years of coaching methodology from João's Surfing Life Club background.

Beyond the surf, the daily rhythm includes [yoga in a forest shala](/yoga-wellness/), surfskate sessions, [creative workshops](/activities/), wine and cheese tastings with local producers, nature hikes, and the inevitable bonfire with conversations that go deeper than you expected. Breakfast is included, always — healthy, with vegetarian, vegan and gluten-free options.

The [accommodation](/accomodation/) is simple but comfortable: private rooms with garden views and private bathrooms (from €80/night), cosy dorms (from €45/night), and glamping tents named after famous surf breaks (Anchor Point, La Gravière) nestled under the pines.

TripAdvisor guests rate the camp 4.8 out of 5. Booking.com gives it a perfect 10 for value. The reviews repeat the same themes: "I came for a week and almost stayed for a month." "More like a communal home than a hostel." "The energy here is unlike anywhere I've stayed."

**The honest trade-off:** Maceda is not a party camp. There's no DJ, no pool, no bar serving cocktails until 2am. The entertainment is a bonfire, a guitar, and the stars. The rooms are clean and comfortable but not luxurious — this is recycled containers and reclaimed wood, not a designer surf lodge. And the location, while spectacularly beautiful, is a quiet village 40 minutes from the nearest city. If you want nightlife, look elsewhere. If you want to wake up to birdsong, surf empty waves, eat grilled fish for €10, and actually feel rested at the end of your holiday, this is the place.

### 2. Golden Waves Surf School — Matosinhos, Porto

**Best for:** City visitors who want to try surfing without committing to a full camp week

Golden Waves operates on Matosinhos beach, the most accessible surf break in the Porto area — you can reach it by subway. The instructors are friendly, the beach is sandy and forgiving, and it's a good option if you're spending a few days in Porto and want to squeeze in a surf lesson between the port wine cellars and the francesinha restaurants.

The trade-off is that Matosinhos gets crowded, especially on summer weekends — you'll share the water with dozens of other beginners, bodyboarders, swimmers and the occasional oblivious paddle-boarder. And the "camp" experience is limited: you book a lesson, show up, surf, and go back to your hotel. There's no community, no communal meals, no bonfire. If you want a taster, it works. If you want immersion, you need something more.

### 3. Oporto Surf Camp — Esmoriz

**Best for:** Social surfers, groups of friends, people who want a lively group atmosphere

Based in Esmoriz, a small coastal town about 25 minutes south of Porto, Oporto Surf Camp has built a solid reputation over the years for its group-oriented vibe. Regular surf lessons on the local beaches, evening social activities, and an atmosphere that runs on communal energy.

The beaches around Esmoriz pick up more swell than some spots further north, which is great for improvers who want waves with a bit more push. On bigger days, though, Esmoriz can produce faster, hollower waves that might overwhelm complete beginners. The accommodation is comfortable and the pricing is fair — typically €300–500 per week depending on the package.

If you're travelling with a group of friends and want a social, active week, this is a solid choice. If you're looking for the quieter, more nature-focused end of the spectrum, it might feel busy.

### 4. Lapoint Surf Camp — Ericeira

**Best for:** Intermediate surfers who want quality waves and a polished, professional experience

Lapoint is a Swedish-founded international surf camp brand with locations across the globe, and their Ericeira operation is one of the most popular in Portugal. The setup is professional: modern accommodation in shared villas, well-structured surf coaching with video analysis, a strong social programme, and the kind of slick branding that appeals to a young professional crowd.

The location is the real selling point. Ericeira is a World Surfing Reserve — one of only a handful on the planet — with a concentration of world-class reef and point breaks within a few kilometres: Coxos, Ribeira d'Ilhas, Cave, São Lourenço. If you're an intermediate surfer who wants to test yourself on proper waves and doesn't mind paying for a premium experience (expect €500–900 per week), Lapoint delivers.

The trade-offs: Ericeira's lineups can be competitive, especially in summer when every surf camp in the area sends their students to the same spots. Localism exists at the better reef breaks. And the pricing reflects Ericeira's status as Portugal's surf capital — this is not the budget end of the spectrum. The town itself is charming, with excellent restaurants and a buzzing nightlife scene, but it's thoroughly discovered at this point. If you were hoping for off-the-beaten-path, Ericeira stopped being that about ten years ago.

### 5. Surf camps in Peniche and Baleal

**Best for:** Wave variety, intermediate to advanced surfers, people who want access to many different breaks

Peniche occupies a peninsula that juts into the Atlantic, creating a unique geographic situation: regardless of the wind or swell direction, there's almost always a wave working somewhere on the peninsula. This makes it one of Portugal's most consistent surf zones. It's also home to Supertubos — one of Europe's heaviest beach breaks and a regular stop on the WSL Championship Tour.

Several camps operate in the area, from budget hostels to more established operations like Baleal Surf Camp. The wave quality is exceptional, with options for every level depending on which side of the peninsula you surf. The downside is that Peniche is popular — very popular — and the lineups can be heavy, especially during summer and competition season. Prices tend to run €400–700 per week.

The town itself is a working fishing port that's gradually becoming more tourist-oriented. It lacks the village charm of Ericeira or the wild nature of northern Portugal, but if your primary criterion is "access to the most waves possible," Peniche is hard to beat.

### 6. Surf camps in the Algarve

**Best for:** Warm water, families, beginners who want sunshine and calm conditions

The Algarve is Portugal's warmest region, with water temperatures reaching 22°C in summer and near-guaranteed sunshine from May to October. The south coast is sheltered and calm; the west coast around Sagres and Arrifana picks up proper Atlantic swell and has some genuinely excellent reef and beach breaks.

Several well-run camps operate down south, including Algarve Surf School and various boutique operations around Lagos and Sagres. The experience is different from the north: warmer, more relaxed, with a resort-town feel in many areas. Families with small children often prefer the Algarve because the water is warmer and the logistics are simpler.

The trade-off is consistency. The swell in the Algarve is less reliable than further north — the south coast can be flat for days, and even the west coast doesn't pick up the same volume of Atlantic swell as the exposed northern coastline. Prices also tend to be higher, reflecting the region's status as Portugal's primary tourist destination.

## How the regions compare

The question "which region?" is really a question about what kind of trip you want.

If you want empty waves, wild nature, authentic Portugal, sustainability and the lowest prices, go north. Porto is on your doorstep for culture and nightlife. The water's colder, yes — but you're wearing a wetsuit regardless, and the emptiness of the lineups more than compensates.

If you want to surf the best waves in the country and don't mind sharing them with a crowd, go to Ericeira or Peniche. The wave quality in central Portugal is world-class. The infrastructure is excellent. You'll pay more, and the experience will feel more touristy, but the surfing itself is hard to argue with.

If you want warmth, sunshine and a gentler introduction, go south. The Algarve delivers on comfort and climate, even if the waves aren't as consistent.

## A note on choosing based on reviews vs reality

Every surf camp in Portugal has good reviews. It's a self-selecting market: people who book surf trips tend to have a great time because the combination of ocean, exercise and community is inherently enjoyable. A 4.5-star average on Google tells you the camp isn't terrible. It doesn't tell you whether you'll actually like it.

The better approach is to match the camp to your personality. Are you the kind of person who wants a bonfire and three close conversations, or a bar and thirty acquaintances? Do you want to wake up to birdsong or music? Do you want a schedule that's structured or one that leaves you alone? Do you care more about the waves or the vibe?

Answer those honestly, and the right camp becomes obvious.
