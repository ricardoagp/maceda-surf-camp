There's a moment on every family holiday when you look at your kids and realise they're actually happy. Not entertained-by-a-screen happy. Not sugar-rush-at-the-pool happy. Actually, genuinely happy — tired, sandy, sunburnt in a vaguely irresponsible way, and talking so fast about the wave they caught that they keep tripping over their own sentences.

That moment is why family surf camps exist. And Portugal — with its warm weather, safe beaches, incredible food, affordable prices and a pace of life that reminds you what holidays felt like before you had to plan every minute — is where most European families end up having it.

A surf camp holiday isn't a resort with a surf school attached. It's something more immersive: your whole family learns something new together, spends the day outdoors, connects with nature and other families, and comes home with stories and skills that last longer than a suntan. It's also, frankly, the kind of holiday where parents get to have a good time too — not just supervise, but participate.

Here's what you need to know.

## Why kids take to surfing (and what they get from it)

Children don't overthink surfing. They don't worry about looking stupid. They don't analyse the wave, calculate the angle, second-guess the timing. They see a wave, they paddle, they stand up (or they don't, and they laugh about it). This lack of self-consciousness is their superpower. Kids pick up surfing faster than adults because they're lighter, more flexible, more fearless and less interested in doing it "correctly" than in doing it at all.

Ask a child who's been to surf camp what they loved, and you'll get a different answer every time. The eight-year-old will tell you about the wave he caught and how he rode it all the way to the beach. The twelve-year-old will talk about the German kid she met who taught her a card game. The six-year-old will say something about a crab he found in a rock pool. What none of them will mention is their phone, their tablet, or anything involving a screen.

That's the quiet miracle of a surf camp holiday. There's so much to do, see, touch and experience that digital devices become irrelevant within hours. Kids rediscover the feeling of being physically tired in a good way, of falling asleep the moment their head hits the pillow, of waking up genuinely excited about what the day holds. They get comfortable in the ocean — not just as a swimming pool with waves, but as a living, changing environment that demands their attention and rewards their courage.

For parents, watching this happen is worth the trip by itself. Seeing your child — the one who "can't" do anything without Wi-Fi, the one who "doesn't like" outdoor activities, the one who's "bored" by everything — suddenly alive, present and radiating enthusiasm. That's what surfing does to kids. It meets them where they are, challenges them exactly enough to be exciting, and gives them a win that feels genuinely earned.

## What a family surf camp holiday actually looks like

### The lessons

Safety comes first, and good surf camps take it seriously. At Maceda, our [surf lessons](/surf/) are taught by qualified, experienced instructors on wide, sandy beaches with gentle waves and no rocks, reef or currents to worry about. The equipment is provided and sized for each person — soft-top boards (which don't hurt when they hit you, and they will hit you), properly fitting wetsuits, and rashguards.

Groups are kept small enough that every student gets genuine attention. This isn't a school assembly where the instructor shouts from the back of the room — it's hands-on coaching, wave by wave, with constant feedback and encouragement.

Kids don't learn like adults, and the instruction reflects that. Younger children — five, six, seven — get shorter sessions focused on fun, water confidence and the thrill of catching whitewater on their belly. The goal isn't perfect technique. It's building a relationship with the ocean that's positive, playful and confidence-building. Most kids at this age won't "surf" in the traditional sense, but they'll splash, ride, tumble and come out of the water grinning from ear to ear.

Older kids and teenagers are different. By eight or nine, most children have the coordination, attention span and physical ability to follow structured instruction and progress quickly. They can learn the pop-up, practise on the sand, apply it in the water, and make meaningful progress over a few sessions. Teenagers are often the fastest learners of all — low body weight, high flexibility, zero fear, and a competitive edge that pushes them to try harder. By the end of a week, a thirteen-year-old who's never surfed before can often catch and ride green (unbroken) waves, which is genuinely impressive.

Parents can surf alongside their kids or in a separate group, depending on everyone's level. Both approaches work. Some families love the bonding of learning together — wiping out together, cheering each other on, comparing notes at lunch. Others prefer to split up so the adults can push themselves while the kids get age-appropriate instruction. There's no wrong answer.

### The rest of the day

Surf lessons typically run in the morning, which leaves the afternoon open. This is where the camp setting earns its keep. A hotel would leave you staring at each other, trying to decide between the pool and the restaurant. A surf camp gives you options that don't require planning or spending money.

At Maceda, the afternoons fill themselves. There's [yoga](/yoga-wellness/) — and before you dismiss that as "not for families," know that kids love yoga when it's presented as play rather than practice. There's surfskate with André, which teenagers in particular gravitate towards. There are [nature walks and workshops](/activities/) (it translates directly to surf technique, and it looks cool). There are nature walks through the pine forest behind the camp, where you'll find trails, wildflowers and the kind of quiet that makes children start whispering without being told to. There's the Barrinha de Esmoriz nature reserve, a freshwater lagoon ten minutes south, with birdlife and wetland ecosystems that make a good afternoon outing.

And there's Porto, 40 minutes north by train, offering everything from the Ribeira waterfront to the port wine cellars (for the adults) to the Serralves Museum and gardens (for everyone). A day trip to Porto is easy, fun and highly recommended — we wrote a [full guide to things to do there](/journal/cool-things-to-do-in-and-around-porto-this-summer/).

Creative workshops happen regularly at camp — ceramics, painting, things that use your hands instead of your thumbs. Wine and cheese tastings (adults only, obviously) use local producers and are as educational as they are enjoyable. And then, as the sun drops, the bonfire. Marshmallows. A guitar. Stories. Kids running around in the garden. Adults talking. Stars appearing. The day ending the way a holiday day should end — slowly, naturally and with everyone already looking forward to tomorrow.

### The food

Mornings start with a communal breakfast: fresh fruit, granola, eggs, bread, juice, good coffee for the adults, and everything you need to fuel a morning in the ocean. The spread always includes vegetarian, vegan and gluten-free options — we're used to feeding families with mixed dietary needs.

For lunch and dinner, the camp has a fully equipped kitchen for self-catering, and the village of Maceda has local restaurants where €10–12 per person gets you a grilled fish plate with salad, bread and wine (or juice for the kids) that would cost three or four times as much in a tourist area. Portuguese food is naturally kid-friendly: grilled chicken, fish, chips, bread, rice. The flavours are simple and honest, not spicy or challenging. Fussy eaters do better here than they expect.

## What age can kids start surfing?

The short answer: most children can start at five or six. The longer answer: it depends on the child.

At five and six, lessons are really about the ocean — getting comfortable in the water, feeling the waves, learning to enjoy the push and pull without fear. Actual surfing (standing up on a board and riding it) comes secondary to building a positive relationship with the sea. Some five-year-olds stand up on their first day. Others spend the session jumping over whitewater and laughing. Both outcomes are equally successful.

By seven or eight, most children have the physical coordination and attention span to follow structured instruction. They can [learn the pop-up](/journal/surfing-northern-portugal-for-beginners-the-ultimate-guide/) on the beach, practise it in the whitewater, and make noticeable progress over a few sessions. This is where the addictive part kicks in — the moment a child stands up on a wave for the first time, you can see the switch flip. They want to do it again. Immediately. And again. And again.

Teenagers are naturals. Low body weight, high flexibility, no fear of failure, and a competitive streak that makes them push harder when they see someone else doing it better. A motivated teenager can go from zero to catching green waves in a week. They also benefit enormously from the social element of camp — meeting other teens from different countries, forming the kind of intense, short-lived friendships that summer holidays are supposed to produce.

If your child is comfortable in the water, not afraid of waves, and willing to try something new, they're ready. That's the only test that matters.

## Practical things parents ask

**Do both parents have to surf?** Not at all. Some parents love learning alongside their kids. Others prefer to let the kids surf while they do yoga, read a book, explore Porto, or simply enjoy the peace of having a few hours to themselves (a luxury that's underrated on family holidays). Both approaches are welcome and normal.

**What if my child doesn't want to surf?** It happens. The ocean can be intimidating, especially for younger or more cautious children. We never force it. There are plenty of other activities at camp — beach play, forest walks, workshops, surfskate — and most reluctant kids, after watching their siblings or other children in the water, decide to try by day two or three. The key is zero pressure.

**Is the camp safe for small children?** The property is enclosed, car-free and surrounded by forest. There are no busy roads, no steep drops, no bodies of water within the camp grounds. Children have room to run, play and explore in a way that feels free for them and safe for you. The beach, when supervised (which it always is during our sessions), is sandy and gentle.

**How much does it cost?** A week for a family of four in a private Garden View room (private bathroom, breakfast included every morning) starts from around €560 for accommodation alone. Surf lesson packages for kids are the same as for adults — from €180 per person for three sessions, or €250 for five. That's your core cost. Add in meals at local restaurants (€10–12 per person per meal), and a full week for a family of four — accommodation, breakfast, surf lessons for two kids, lunches and dinners out — comes in somewhere around €1,200–1,500. That's a fraction of what a comparable experience would cost in the Algarve or at a resort anywhere in southern Europe.

**Can we combine it with a city trip?** Absolutely. Porto is 40 minutes by train, and it's one of Europe's most family-friendly cities. The Ribeira waterfront, the tram along the Douro, the Serralves gardens, the port wine cellars (for adults — the kids can stay in the gardens), and enough francesinhas to keep everyone happy. Many families spend two or three days in Porto before or after their camp week.
